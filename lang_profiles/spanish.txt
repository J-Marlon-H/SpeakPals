# Language Background: Spanish → Danish

## How to use this profile
Spanish and Danish share almost no direct vocabulary — they come from entirely different branches
(Romance vs. North Germanic). However, Spanish speakers bring real strengths: strong phonetic
awareness, good intuition for grammatical gender, and a culture of expressive communication.
Build on these. Expect the pronunciation gaps and the V2 word order to be the hardest walls.

## A2 — Grammar comparisons for Spanish speakers

### What is EASIER in Danish than Spanish:
- **Verb conjugation:** Spanish: como / comes / come / comemos / coméis / comen →
  Danish: spiser / spiser / spiser — same form for all persons. Relief for Spanish speakers.
- **No subjunctive mood at A2:** Danish subjunctive is rare; don't worry about it early.
- **No complex verb tenses:** Danish past is simple (spiste) vs. Spanish preterite/imperfect split.
- **No gendered adjectives:** Danish adjectives don't change form for gender/person.

### What is HARDER — explain these carefully:

**1. Grammatical gender: en/et (not masculine/feminine)**
Spanish has masculine (el/un) and feminine (la/una). Danish has common (en) and neuter (et).
The split does not follow the same pattern — "en mand" (a man) is common, "et barn" (a child) is neuter.
The article must be memorised with each noun.
Teaching tip: always teach noun + article as a pair.

**2. Definite article as a suffix**
Spanish uses a separate article: el perro (the dog). Danish attaches it to the end:
- en hund (a dog) → hunden (the dog)
- et hus (a house) → huset (the house)
Teaching tip: practise "a dog / the dog" pairs from day one.

**3. V2 word order (verb second)**
Spanish is flexible with SVO: "Ayer fui al colegio" is natural.
Danish requires the verb second: "I går gik jeg i skole." (verb must come second in the sentence)
This is the most persistent error for Spanish speakers — they put the subject first instinctively.

**4. Negation position**
Spanish: "No como carne." (no before verb)
Danish: "Jeg spiser ikke kød." (ikke after the verb)

**5. Stød (glottal stop)**
No equivalent in Spanish. A creaky break in the voice that changes word meaning.
"Hund" (dog) vs. "hun" (she) — distinguished partly by stød. Train the ear early; speaking
approximation is acceptable at A2.

### A2 Key grammar structures to practise:
- Present tense: spise → jeg spiser, du spiser, han spiser (same for all persons)
- Past tense: spiste (regular), var (was), fik (got), gik (went)
- Question formation: Spiser du? (verb first — same inversion logic as Spanish ¿Comes tú?)
- Negation: ikke (not), aldrig (never), ingen (none)

### A2 Vocabulary expansion — everyday life:
**Transport:** bus, tog (train), cykel (bicycle), bil (car), fly (plane)
**Shopping:** butik (shop), pris (price), billig (cheap), dyr (expensive), købe (to buy)
**Time:** i dag (today), i morgen (tomorrow), i går (yesterday), nu (now), snart (soon)
**Weather:** sol (sun), regn (rain), sne (snow), vind (wind), koldt (cold), varmt (warm)
**Home:** køkken (kitchen), stue (living room), soveværelse (bedroom), badeværelse (bathroom)

## B1 — Advanced connections and nuances

### Idiomatic expressions (with Spanish equivalents):
- "Det er lige meget" = "Da igual" (it doesn't matter)
- "Hvad så?" = "¿Y qué?" / "¿Qué tal?" (so what / what's up)
- "Gå op i røg" = "Irse al traste" (go up in smoke — similar concept)
- "Slå to fluer med ét smæk" = "Matar dos pájaros de un tiro" (kill two birds — identical concept!)
- "Tage det roligt" = "Tomárselo con calma" (take it easy)
- "Koste en arm og et ben" = "Costar un ojo de la cara" (cost a fortune — same concept, different body parts)
- "Gøre en bjørnetjeneste" = "Hacer un flaco favor" (do a disservice)
- "Komme til sagen" = "Ir al grano" (get to the point)

### B1 Grammar nuances:
- **Passive voice:** Bogen læses (The book is read) vs. Bogen bliver læst (active passive)
- **Modal verbs in past:** kunne (could), måtte (had to), ville (wanted to), skulle (was supposed to)
- **Relative clauses:** "Manden, som/der bor her..." (el hombre que vive aquí)
- **Subordinate clause word order:** "Jeg ved, at han ikke kommer" — ikke comes BEFORE the verb in subordinate clauses (opposite of main clause order)

### B1 Register — formal vs. informal:
- De (formal you) vs. du (informal) — similar concept to usted vs. tú
- Danish informal register is extremely common even in professional settings — Danes use du universally

### False friends — warn the student immediately:
| Danish | What it means | Spanish false friend |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| gift | married / poison | regalo (gift = present in Spanish) |
| fart | speed | pedo (fart in Spanish) |
| mand | man / husband | man (same, actually fine) |
| have | garden | haber/tener (have in Spanish) |
| bad | bath | malo (bad in Spanish) |
| blive | to stay / to become | vivir (sounds loosely like) |
| eller | or | ella (she in Spanish — sounds close) |
| slut | end / finish | slut (English false friend worth flagging) |

### Pronunciation guide for Spanish speakers:
- **Soft d (blødt d):** Between vowels, sounds like a very soft English "th" in "the". Similar to
  Spanish intervocalic "d" in "nada" — Spanish speakers often get this right instinctively. Use this!
- **Stød (glottal stop):** No equivalent in Spanish. Describe it as a brief catch in the throat.
  Drill: hund, mand, sol with stød variants.
- **å sound:** Spanish speakers: say the "o" in "sol" but with rounded lips pushed further forward.
  Practice: å, år, gå, stå, kål.
- **ø sound:** No equivalent in Spanish. Say the "e" in "mesa" while rounding your lips as if saying "o".
  Practice: grøn, øl, søster, størrelse.
- **æ sound:** Similar to the "e" in "mesa" but longer and more open. Practice: læse, æble.
- **Danish r:** Not rolled like Spanish r. It's a soft, guttural sound made at the back of the throat.
  Spanish speakers must unlearn the roll — it sounds wrong in Danish.
