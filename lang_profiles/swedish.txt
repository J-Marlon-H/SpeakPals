# Language Background: Swedish → Danish

## How to use this profile
Swedish speakers have the greatest advantage of any group. Written Danish and Swedish are largely mutually
intelligible. The student can skip many A1 basics and move faster — but spoken Danish is a genuine challenge
and needs dedicated focus. Do not let the false sense of familiarity prevent drilling pronunciation.

### A1 Greetings (minimal teaching needed):
Swedish speakers understand most Danish greetings intuitively:
- Hej (SW: hej) = Hej (DA) — identical
- Hejdå / Adjö (SW) → Hej hej / Farvel (DA) — slightly different
- Tack (SW) → Tak (DA) — very close
- Varsågod (SW) → Vær så venlig / Selv tak (DA)
- Ja / Nej — identical

### A1 Numbers (almost identical — teach differences only):
SW: ett, två, tre, fyra, fem, sex, sju, åtta, nio, tio
DA: en/et, to, tre, fire, fem, seks, syv, otte, ni, ti
Focus on: to (not "två"), syv (not "sju"), otte (not "åtta")

## A2 — Grammar comparisons for Swedish speakers

### What is IDENTICAL — already mastered:

**1. Gender system: en/ett = en/et**
Swedish: en hund, ett hus → Danish: en hund, et hus
The logic is identical. Definite form also attaches as suffix.
Swedish: hunden, huset → Danish: hunden, huset (same!)

**2. No person conjugation:**
Swedish: jag går, du går, han går → Danish: jeg går, du går, han går
Identical structure — verb never changes by person.

**3. V2 word order:**
Swedish: Igår gick jag till skolan. → Danish: I går gik jeg i skole.
The V2 rule (verb second in main clause) is the same.

**4. Suffix definite articles:**
Swedish: boken, huset, stolen → Danish: bogen, huset, stolen
Near-identical! The plural definite differs slightly:
SW: hundarna → DA: hundene (different suffix)

### What is DIFFERENT — these need drilling:

**1. Past tense formation — major difference:**
Swedish strong verbs: gick (went), såg (saw), fick (got), kom (came)
Danish strong verbs: gik (went), så (saw), fik (got), kom (came)
Very close but not identical — many students mix up the spelling.

**2. Subordinate clause: "ikke" position**
Danish: "Jeg ved, at han ikke kommer." (ikke before verb)
Swedish: "Jag vet att han inte kommer." (same position — reassure student, it IS the same!)

**3. Definiteness with adjectives:**
Swedish: den store hunden / det stora huset
Danish: den store hund / det store hus (adjective changes, noun does NOT add suffix when adjective present!)
This is a key Danish rule: definite article + adjective + noun (no suffix on noun).

**4. Plural forms differ:**
Swedish: hundar, katter, böcker → Danish: hunde, katte, bøger
The concept is the same but suffixes differ — drill the Danish forms specifically.

## B1 — Advanced connections and nuances

### Idiomatic expressions (with Swedish equivalents):
- "Det er lige meget" = "Det spelar ingen roll" (it doesn't matter)
- "Slå to fluer med ét smæk" = "Slå två flugor i en smäll" (exactly the same idiom!)
- "Gå op i røg" = "Gå upp i rök" (go up in smoke — same!)
- "Koste en arm og et ben" = "Kosta skjortan" (cost a lot — different image)
- "Tage det roligt" = "Ta det lugnt" (take it easy — same structure!)
- "Komme til sagen" = "Komma till saken" (get to the point — identical!)
- "Have ret" = "Ha rätt" (be right — identical structure!)
- "Det er ikke min kop te" = "Det är inte min kopp te" (identical!)
- "Gøre en bjørnetjeneste" = "Göra en björntjänst" (exactly the same idiom!)
- "Se frem til" = "Se fram emot" (look forward to — very close!)
- "Sætte spørgsmålstegn ved" = "Ifrågasätta" (to question)

### B1 Grammar nuances — key divergences from Swedish:

**1. Passive voice:**
Danish: læse → læses (passive) / bliver læst (analytical passive)
Swedish: läsa → läses / blir läst — very similar, reassure student

**2. Perfect tense:**
Danish: Jeg har spist / Jeg har set
Swedish: Jag har ätit / Jag har sett — same structure, different past participles

**3. The definite form with attributive adjective (IMPORTANT):**
SW: "Jag ser den gamla mannen" (den + adj + noun with no extra suffix)
DA: "Jeg ser den gamle mand" (den + adj + mand — no extra suffix — SAME rule!)
Students often add -en/-et to the noun unnecessarily — remind them not to.

**4. Modal verbs in past — slight differences:**
SW: kunde, måste, ville, skulle
DA: kunne, måtte, ville, skulle — almost identical, just remind student of spelling.

**5. Reflexive verbs:**
SW: Jag tvättar mig / Vi ses
DA: Jeg vasker mig / Vi ses — same structure!

### Pronunciation guide for Swedish speakers — THE CRITICAL AREA:

Written Danish: easy. Spoken Danish: a genuine challenge. Be direct with the student about this.

**1. Soft d (blødt d) — does not exist in Swedish:**
Between vowels, Danish d sounds like the English "th" in "the".
Swedish speakers say a clear Swedish "d" — must be corrected immediately.
Drill words: mad (food), rød (red), glad (happy), side (page), bade (to bathe), hede (heath).

**2. Stød vs Swedish pitch accent — completely different systems:**
Swedish has tonal word accent (tone 1 vs tone 2) — this does NOT transfer to Danish.
Danish has stød (glottal stop/creak) instead. "Hund" vs "hun" (she) — stød changes meaning.
Tell students: forget Swedish tones entirely when speaking Danish.

**3. Vowel reduction — far more extreme than Swedish:**
Danish reduces and swallows vowels aggressively.
"Hvad hedder du?" sounds approximately like "veheddu?" in fast speech.
"Selvfølgelig" → "selfolli"
"Jeg ved det ikke" → "jæ ve dedge"
Train the student to listen to natural speech, not spelled-out pronunciation.

**4. The silent letters:**
- hv-words: hvad, hvor, hvem, hvilken — the h is silent. Say "vad", "vor", "vem".
- d at end of word after vowel: rød → approximately "røth", glad → approximately "glath"
- g after vowel often silent or very weak: dag → "da", sag → "sa"

**5. Å, Ø, Æ — Swedish speakers know these sounds:**
- å = same as Swedish å: år, gå, stå ✓
- ø = same as Swedish ö: grøn, øl, søster ✓
- æ = same as Swedish ä: læse, æble ✓
This is a major advantage — these vowels are already natural.

**6. The Danish "r":**
Danish r is uvular (back of throat), like French r — not the Swedish rolled r.
Students must consciously place it further back: rød, rose, ret, rejse.
