# Language Background: Dutch → Danish

## How to use this profile
Dutch speakers have the second-largest head start after Germans. Dutch and Danish are both Low
Germanic languages and share enormous vocabulary overlap — a Dutch speaker can often read a Danish
sentence and understand it immediately. Move FAST at A1. The shared grammar structures (V2 word
order, definite suffix concept, de/het ≈ en/et) mean the student can focus almost entirely on
vocabulary and pronunciation from day one. Pronunciation is the main challenge — the two languages
sound very different despite sharing so much in writing.

## A2 — Grammar comparisons for Dutch speakers

### What is EASIER — and already familiar from Dutch:

**1. V2 word order — Dutch speakers already know this**
Dutch: "Gisteren ging ik naar school." (verb second after fronted element)
Danish: "I går gik jeg i skole." — identical rule. This is a major advantage.
Teach it as: "You already do this in Dutch — same rule."

**2. Grammatical gender concept: en/et ≈ de/het**
Dutch has de (common) and het (neuter). Danish has en (common) and et (neuter).
The split is not identical but the CONCEPT is the same — two gender classes, one for most nouns,
one for neuter nouns. Dutch speakers understand this immediately.
Note: the actual assignment differs — "de hond" (Dutch) = "en hund" (Danish), same class.
But "het huis" (Dutch) = "et hus" (Danish) — both neuter. Significant overlap.

**3. Definite article as suffix — partially familiar**
Dutch attaches "-de/-te" for diminutives and has some suffix patterns. Danish attaches the definite article
as a full suffix: hund → hunden, hus → huset. The concept of attaching morphology to nouns is natural for Dutch speakers.

**4. Negation position**
Dutch: "Ik eet geen vlees." / "Ik eet het vlees niet."
Danish: "Jeg spiser ikke kød." — ikke after the verb, same structural position as Dutch "niet".

### What is still HARDER:

**1. Stød (glottal stop)**
Dutch has no stød. A creaky glottal stop that changes word meaning in Danish.
No Dutch equivalent — closest analogy is the pause between vowels in "beamen" (be-amen).
Drill: hund, mand, sol — expose early, expect approximation only at A1–A2.

**2. Pronunciation divergence**
Despite shared vocabulary, Dutch and Danish sound very different. Dutch is harder-edged with
clear consonants. Danish swallows syllables, has the soft d, and the stød. The student may
be surprised how different it sounds after reading so much successfully.

**3. Vocabulary where Dutch and Danish diverged**
Some core words differ entirely despite shared roots:
- Dutch: eten (to eat) → Danish: spise (to eat)
- Dutch: zien (to see) → Danish: se (to see — actually close!)
- Dutch: spreken (to speak) → Danish: tale/snakke
- Dutch: lopen (to walk) → Danish: gå
Teach these active differences explicitly.

### A2 Key grammar structures to practise:
- Present tense: spise → jeg spiser, du spiser, han spiser (same for all persons — simpler than Dutch)
- Past tense: spiste (regular), var (was), fik (got), gik (went)
- Question formation: Spiser du? (inversion — same as Dutch: Eet jij?)
- Negation: ikke (not), aldrig (never), ingen (none)

### A2 Vocabulary expansion — everyday life:
**Transport:** bus, tog (train — Dutch: trein), cykel (bicycle — Dutch: fiets), bil (car — Dutch: auto), fly (plane — Dutch: vliegtuig)
**Shopping:** butik (shop), pris (prijs — close!), billig (cheap), dyr (expensive — Dutch: duur, same root!), købe (to buy)
**Time:** i dag (today — Dutch: vandaag), i morgen (tomorrow — Dutch: morgen, same root!), i går (yesterday — Dutch: gisteren), nu (now — Dutch: nu, identical!)
**Weather:** sol (zon — close!), regn (regen — near-identical!), sne (sneeuw — same root), vind (wind — identical!), koldt (koud — same root), varmt (warm — same root)

## B1 — Advanced connections and nuances

### Idiomatic expressions (with Dutch equivalents):
- "Det er lige meget" = "Het maakt niet uit" (it doesn't matter)
- "Hvad så?" = "En dan?" / "En nu?" (so what / what's up)
- "Gå op i røg" = "In rook opgaan" (go up in smoke — same idiom, same structure!)
- "Slå to fluer med ét smæk" = "Twee vliegen in één klap slaan" (kill two birds — near-identical!)
- "Tage det roligt" = "Het rustig aan doen" (take it easy)
- "Koste en arm og et ben" = "Een vermogen kosten" (cost a fortune)
- "Komme til sagen" = "Ter zake komen" (get to the point — same structure!)
- "Gøre en bjørnetjeneste" = "Een berendienst bewijzen" (do a disservice — same bear idiom!)

### B1 Grammar nuances:
- **Passive voice:** Bogen læses vs. Bogen bliver læst — same two-form passive as Dutch (word wordt gelezen / het boek is gelezen)
- **Modal verbs in past:** kunne (kon), måtte (moest), ville (wilde), skulle (moest/zou) — close to Dutch equivalents
- **Relative clauses:** "Manden, som/der bor her..." = "De man die hier woont..." — same structure
- **Subordinate clause word order:** "Jeg ved, at han ikke kommer" — ikke before verb in subordinate clauses,
  same as Dutch: "Ik weet dat hij niet komt."

### B1 Register — formal vs. informal:
- De (formal you) vs. du (informal) — same concept as Dutch U vs. jij/je
- Danish informal (du) is universal even in professional settings — more so than Dutch

### False friends — warn the student immediately:
| Danish | What it means | Dutch false friend |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| gift | married / poison | gift (in Dutch = poison too! — actually a TRUE friend in the poison sense) |
| fart | speed | vaart (speed/pace — actually a true friend! Both mean speed) |
| have | garden | hebben (to have — different meaning) |
| bad | bath | bad (bad = bath in Dutch too — TRUE friend!) |
| kind | cheek | kind (child in Dutch — false friend, means cheek in Danish) |
| mand | man / husband | man (man — true friend) |
| gå | to walk/go | gaan (to go — true friend!) |
| stå | to stand | staan (to stand — true friend!) |
| se | to see | zien / zien (to see — close enough) |
| dag | day | dag (day — identical, AND goodbye in Dutch) |

### Pronunciation guide for Dutch speakers:
- **Soft d (blødt d):** Between vowels, Danish d sounds like English "th" in "the". Dutch d in
  similar positions is harder. Example: "mad" (food) → sounds like "math". Dutch speakers need
  to soften the d significantly.
- **Stød (glottal stop):** No equivalent in Dutch. Brief creaky catch in the voice.
  Drill: hund, mand, sol. Tell the student: think of it as a tiny pause with a creak — not a full stop.
- **å sound:** Like Dutch "oo" in "boot" but more open and forward. Practice: å, år, gå, stå.
- **ø sound:** Similar to Dutch "eu" in "neus" or "deur". Dutch speakers usually produce this well.
  Use: "grøn sounds like Dutch 'greun', øl like Dutch 'eul'." Confidence booster.
- **æ sound:** Similar to Dutch "ee" in open syllables or "e" in "bed". Practice: læse, æble.
- **Danish r:** Guttural like Dutch r (both uvular). Dutch speakers usually produce this correctly.
  Another confidence booster — point this out early.
- **Swallowed syllables:** Danish drops far more sounds than Dutch in natural speech.
  "Selvfølgelig" (of course) sounds like "sel-fol-li". Train the ear — don't read, listen.
- **Hard vs. soft consonants:** Danish consonants are generally softer/more voiced than Dutch.
  Dutch "p/t/k" are strongly aspirated; Danish versions are much softer.
