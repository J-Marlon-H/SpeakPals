# Language Background: French → Danish

## How to use this profile
French is a Romance language — distant from Danish grammatically and lexically. However, French
speakers are typically strong analytical learners with solid grammar intuition, and there are more
cognates than one would expect (via shared Latin roots and Norman French influence on both English
and, indirectly, Danish). Move at a measured pace on pronunciation — French and Danish sound worlds
apart, but French speakers tend to be motivated and methodical.

## A2 — Grammar comparisons for French speakers

### What is EASIER in Danish than French:
- **Verb conjugation:** French: je mange / tu manges / il mange / nous mangeons / vous mangez / ils mangent →
  Danish: spiser / spiser / spiser — one form for all persons. Major simplification.
- **No subjunctive to worry about at A2:** Danish subjunctive is archaic and rare.
- **No complex tense splits:** No imparfait vs. passé composé distinction — Danish past is simpler.
- **Simpler adjective agreement:** Danish adjectives don't change for gender/person in most cases.
- **Definite article concept:** French uses a separate article (le chien). Danish has a suffix (hunden).
  Different form, but French speakers are used to thinking about definiteness explicitly.

### What is HARDER — explain these carefully:

**1. Grammatical gender: en/et (not masculine/feminine)**
French has masculine (le/un) and feminine (la/une). Danish has common (en) and neuter (et).
The split does not map to French gender — "en mand" (man) is common, "et barn" (child) is neuter.
Must be memorised with each noun.
Teaching tip: always teach noun + article together.

**2. Definite article as a suffix**
French: le chien (the dog) — separate article before the noun.
Danish: hunden (the dog) — suffix added to the end.
- en hund (a dog) → hunden (the dog)
- et hus (a house) → huset (the house)

**3. V2 word order (verb second)**
French: "Hier je suis allé à l'école." (subject comes first naturally)
Danish: "I går gik jeg i skole." (verb must be second — subject inverts after any fronted element)
This feels unnatural to French speakers. It's the same rule as German, which French speakers may know.

**4. Negation position**
French: "Je ne mange pas de viande." (ne...pas around verb)
Danish: "Jeg spiser ikke kød." (ikke after the verb, no double negation)

**5. Stød (glottal stop)**
No equivalent in French. Describe as a brief creaky catch in the voice.
No French word has this — closest is a glottal stop between vowels in careful Parisian speech.

### A2 Key grammar structures to practise:
- Present tense: spise → jeg spiser, du spiser, han spiser (same for all persons)
- Past tense: spiste (regular), var (was), fik (got), gik (went)
- Question formation: Spiser du? (verb first — inversion, like French "Manges-tu?")
- Negation: ikke (not), aldrig (never), ingen (none)

### A2 Vocabulary expansion — everyday life:
**Transport:** bus, tog (train), cykel (bicycle), bil (car), fly (plane)
**Shopping:** butik (shop), pris (price), billig (cheap), dyr (expensive), købe (to buy)
**Time:** i dag (today), i morgen (tomorrow), i går (yesterday), nu (now), snart (soon)
**Weather:** sol (sun), regn (rain), sne (snow), vind (wind), koldt (cold), varmt (warm)
**Home:** køkken (kitchen), stue (living room), soveværelse (bedroom), badeværelse (bathroom)

## B1 — Advanced connections and nuances

### Idiomatic expressions (with French equivalents):
- "Det er lige meget" = "Ça ne fait rien" / "Peu importe" (it doesn't matter)
- "Hvad så?" = "Et alors?" (so what / what's up)
- "Gå op i røg" = "Partir en fumée" (go up in smoke — same idiom!)
- "Slå to fluer med ét smæk" = "Faire d'une pierre deux coups" (kill two birds with one stone — same concept)
- "Tage det roligt" = "Prendre les choses calmement" (take it easy)
- "Gøre en bjørnetjeneste" = "Rendre un mauvais service" (do a disservice)
- "Komme til sagen" = "Aller au fait" / "Entrer dans le vif du sujet" (get to the point)
- "Koste en arm og et ben" = "Coûter les yeux de la tête" (cost a fortune)

### B1 Grammar nuances:
- **Passive voice:** Bogen læses (The book is read) vs. Bogen bliver læst (active passive)
- **Modal verbs in past:** kunne (could/pouvait), måtte (had to/devait), ville (wanted to/voulait), skulle (was supposed to/devait)
- **Relative clauses:** "Manden, som/der bor her..." = "L'homme qui habite ici..."
- **Subordinate clause word order:** "Jeg ved, at han ikke kommer" — ikke comes BEFORE the verb
  in subordinate clauses. French: "Je sais qu'il ne vient pas" — similar position concept.

### B1 Register — formal vs. informal:
- De (formal you) vs. du (informal) — same concept as vous vs. tu
- Danish informal (du) is used even in formal professional settings — far more than French tu

### False friends — warn the student immediately:
| Danish | What it means | French false friend |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| gift | married / poison | cadeau (gift = present in French) |
| fart | speed | pet (fart in French — flagging for awareness) |
| have | garden | avoir (to have in French — totally different) |
| bad | bath | mauvais (bad in French) |
| blive | to stay / to become | (no direct false friend, but sounds vaguely like "vivre") |
| mand | man / husband | (same concept — no false friend issue) |
| slut | end / finish | (English false friend worth flagging) |
| plads | space / seat / square | place (same root — actually a true friend!) |
| station | station | station (true friend — identical!) |

### Pronunciation guide for French speakers:
- **Soft d (blødt d):** Between vowels, sounds like English "th" in "the". French speakers tend
  to place it as a hard d — needs drilling. Example: "mad" (food) → sounds like "math".
- **Stød (glottal stop):** No equivalent in French. Explain as a brief stop in the throat.
  Drill words: hund, mand, sol (with stød versions). Don't stress it at A1–A2.
- **å sound:** Similar to the French "o" in "or" / "côte" — rounded and back. Practice: å, år, gå.
- **ø sound:** French speakers have a huge advantage — ø is identical to French "eu" in "feu" or
  "peu". Use this immediately: "grøn sounds like French 'greun', øl like 'eul'". This is one area
  where French speakers outperform all others.
- **æ sound:** Similar to French "è" in "fête" or "même". Practice: læse, æble.
- **Danish r:** Guttural, made at the back of the throat — very similar to French r. French speakers
  often produce this correctly with little instruction. Use this as a confidence booster.
- **Nasal vowels:** French has nasal vowels (in, un, on, an). Danish does NOT. French speakers may
  nasalise where they shouldn't. Drill: ingen, men, han — no nasalisation.
