# Language Background: German → Danish

## How to use this profile
German speakers have the largest head start of any group here. The languages share North/West Germanic roots,
very similar grammar structures, and massive vocabulary overlap. Move faster than with English speakers at A1 —
the student can absorb more. Focus energy on pronunciation, which diverges sharply.

## A2 — Grammar comparisons for German speakers

### What is EASIER in Danish than German — celebrate these!

**1. Only 2 genders instead of 3**
German: der/die/das → Danish: en/et
No more neuter/masculine/feminine split — just common (en) vs neuter (et).
Teaching tip: "Denk nicht an der/die/das — nur en oder et!"

**2. No case system**
German: Nominativ, Akkusativ, Dativ, Genitiv with changing articles
Danish: no cases — the article never changes form based on sentence role.
"Ich gebe dem Mann das Buch" → "Jeg giver manden bogen" (no case changes)

**3. Simpler verb conjugation**
German: ich gehe / du gehst / er geht / wir gehen / ihr geht / sie gehen
Danish: jeg går / du går / han går / vi går / I går / de går (SAME FORM throughout!)

### What is HARDER — explain these:

**1. Definite article as suffix**
German uses separate articles: der Mann, das Haus
Danish attaches them: manden (the man), huset (the house)
- en mand (a man) → manden (the man)
- et hus (a house) → huset (the house)
- en hund (a dog) → hunden (the dog)
- et barn (a child) → barnet (the child)

**2. Subordinate clause word order — different from German!**
German sub-clause: verb goes to the END — "Ich weiß, dass er kommt."
Danish sub-clause: "ikke" (not) goes BEFORE the verb — "Jeg ved, at han ikke kommer."
(In German this would be: "Ich weiß, dass er nicht kommt." — similar position but different rule)

**3. Main clause word order (V2) — same as German!**
"Gestern bin ich zur Schule gegangen" → "I går gik jeg i skole"
The verb is always second — exactly like German. This is a big advantage.

**4. Separable verbs — same concept, different style:**
German: aufmachen (to open) — ich mache auf
Danish: åbne is simple; but: fortælle (fortæl-ler), komme an (arrive)
Not as systematic as German, but the concept of verb+particle exists.

### A2 Vocabulary expansion — everyday life:

**At the shop:** butik (Laden), pris (Preis), billig (billig — same!), dyr (teuer), købe (kaufen), betale (bezahlen)
**Transport:** tog (Zug), cykel (Fahrrad), bil (Auto/Wagen), fly (Flugzeug)
**Weather:** sol (Sonne), regn (Regen — close!), sne (Schnee — close!), vind (Wind — same!), koldt (kalt), varmt (warm — same!)
**Home:** køkken (Küche), stue (Wohnzimmer), soveværelse (Schlafzimmer), badeværelse (Badezimmer)
**Time:** i dag (heute), i morgen (morgen), i går (gestern), nu (jetzt), snart (bald)

### Compound nouns — German speakers already get this:
Danish loves compounds just like German:
- Jernbane (Eisenbahn — railway): jern (iron) + bane (track)
- Fodgænger (Fußgänger — pedestrian): fod (foot) + gænger (goer)
- Håndtaske (Handtasche — handbag): hånd (hand) + taske (bag)
- Arbejdsplads (Arbeitsplatz — workplace): arbejds (work) + plads (place)

## B1 — Advanced connections and nuances

### Idiomatic expressions (with German equivalents):
- "Det er lige meget" = "Das ist egal" (it doesn't matter)
- "Slå to fluer med ét smæk" = "Zwei Fliegen mit einer Klappe schlagen" (exact same idiom!)
- "Gå op i røg" = "In Rauch aufgehen" (go up in smoke — same!)
- "Koste en arm og et ben" = "Ein Vermögen kosten" (cost a fortune)
- "Tage det roligt" = "Es ruhig angehen lassen" (take it easy)
- "Komme til sagen" = "Zur Sache kommen" (get to the point — nearly identical!)
- "Gøre en bjørnetjeneste" = "Jemandem einen Bärendienst erweisen" (exactly the same!)
- "Have ret" = "Recht haben" (to be right — identical structure!)
- "Det er ikke min kop te" = "Das ist nicht mein Fall" (not my thing)
- "Sætte spørgsmålstegn ved" = "In Frage stellen" (to question something)

### B1 Grammar nuances:
- **Passive voice:** Bogen læses (Das Buch wird gelesen) / Bogen blev læst (Das Buch wurde gelesen)
- **Perfect tense:** Jeg har spist (Ich habe gegessen) — same auxiliary "have/haben"!
- **Modal verbs in past:** kunne (konnte), måtte (musste), ville (wollte), skulle (sollte)
- **Relative clauses:** "Manden, der bor her..." = "Der Mann, der hier wohnt..." (very similar)
- **Conditional:** "Hvis jeg var rig, ville jeg rejse" = "Wenn ich reich wäre, würde ich reisen" (same structure!)

### B1 Register — formal vs. informal:
- Danish is far more informal than German in everyday speech
- "Du" is used with almost everyone — no "Sie/Ihr" distinction needed in practice
- First names are used universally, even with bosses and strangers
- This informality can surprise German speakers — it is not rude, it is Danish culture

### False friends — warn the student:
| Danish | What it means | German confusion |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| gift | married / poison | Gift (Ger.) = poison ✓ but also means "married"! |
| fart | speed | Fahrt (journey) — similar false friend for Germans |
| blive | to become / to stay | bleiben (stay) — close but not identical! |
| siden | since / ago | seitdem — similar |
| rar | nice/kind | rar (Ger.) = rare — different! |
| mænd | men (plural of mand) | Männer — recognisable |
| kede sig | to be bored | sich langweilen — different construction |
| synes | to think/have an opinion | scheinen (seem) — partial overlap |
| egentlig | actually/really | eigentlich — same meaning! |
| selvfølgelig | of course | selbstverständlich — same meaning, recognisable root |

### B1 Common collocations:
- tage en beslutning (eine Entscheidung treffen — make a decision)
- gøre en forskel (einen Unterschied machen — make a difference)
- have ret (Recht haben — to be right)
- være glad for (sich freuen über — be happy about)
- holde op med (aufhören mit — stop doing something)
- komme overens med (auskommen mit — get along with)
- se frem til (sich freuen auf — look forward to)

### Pronunciation guide for German speakers:
- **Soft d (blødt d):** The Danish d between vowels is NOT a German d or t — it sounds like the English "th" in "the". German speakers typically say a hard "d" — must drill this. Words: mad (food), rød (red), glad (happy), bade (to bathe).
- **Stød (glottal stop):** A creaky laryngeal break. No German equivalent. "Hund" with stød vs "hun" (she) without — it changes meaning. Practice: hund, sol, hjem.
- **Reduced pronunciation:** Danish swallows syllables far more than German. "Selvfølgelig" sounds like "selfolli". "Hvad hedder du?" sounds like "veheddu?". Trust your ears more than the spelling.
- **å:** Like German "o" in "offen" but rounder. gå, stå, år, kål.
- **ø:** Like German "ö". grøn, øl, søster.
- **æ:** Like German "ä". læse, æble.
- **The h in hv-words:** "hvad", "hvor", "hvem" — the h is NOT pronounced. Say "vad", "vor", "vem".
