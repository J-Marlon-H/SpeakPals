# Language Background: German

## Similarities to Danish
- Both are North/West Germanic languages -- the largest overlap of any pair here.
- Many cognates: "Haus" = "hus", "Wasser" = "vand", "Winter" = "vinter", "Brot" = "broed".
- Both have grammatical gender, though Danish has two (common/neuter) vs German's three (m/f/n).
- Both use compound nouns freely: "Jernbane" (railway) mirrors the German compounding style.
- Modal verbs behave similarly: "kann/kan", "muss/maa", "will/vil".

## Key Differences to Highlight
- Danish gender is simpler -- only en/et instead of der/die/das.
- Danish has no case system (no Nominativ/Akkusativ/Dativ/Genitiv distinctions for articles).
- Danish word order in main clauses is similar (V2 rule), but subordinate clause word order differs from German.
- Danish pronunciation is considerably more reduced than German -- many consonants are swallowed or softened.
- The soft d (like English "the") and the stoed (glottal stop) are Danish-specific sounds.

## Teaching Tips for German Speakers
- Celebrate the simpler article and case system as a relief.
- Map German strong verbs to Danish equivalents early.
- Warn that written Danish looks more familiar than spoken Danish sounds.
- Drill the soft d -- German speakers tend to pronounce it as a hard d or t.
