# Language Background: English → Danish

## How to use this profile
Use these insights to speed up the student's learning by connecting Danish to what they already know from English.
Point out cognates immediately — they are confidence boosters. Flag false friends early to prevent embarrassment.

### A1 Core vocabulary by topic

**Greetings:**
- Hello = Hej (sounds like "hi")
- Good morning = Godmorgen

**Colors:**

- bread = brød | milk = mælk | water = vand | beer = øl

## A2 — Grammar comparisons for English speakers

### What is EASIER in Danish than English:
- **Verb conjugation:** English: "I am / you are / he is" → Danish: jeg er / du er / han er (same form always)
- **No irregular plurals:** mostly add -er or -e: dog = hund → hunde
- **No genitive case:** just add -s like English: the dog's = hundens

### What is HARDER — explain these carefully:

**1. Grammatical gender (en/et)**
English has no gender. Danish has two: common (en) and neuter (et).
The article must be memorised with each noun — there is no rule.
- en hund (a dog) → hunden (the dog)
- et hus (a house) → huset (the house)
Teaching tip: always teach noun + article as a pair, never the noun alone.

**2. Definite article as suffix**
English uses a separate word "the". Danish attaches it to the end of the noun:
- the dog = hunden (en hund + en)
- the house = huset (et hus + et)
- the dogs = hundene

**3. Verb-second (V2) word order**
English: "Yesterday I went to school."
Danish: "I går gik jeg i skole." (verb must be second — subject moves after verb)
This is the same as German but different from English.

**4. Negation position**
English: "I do not eat meat." → Danish: "Jeg spiser ikke kød." (ikke after verb)

### A2 Key grammar structures to practise:
- Present tense: spise → jeg spiser, du spiser, han spiser
- Past tense: spiste (regular), var (was), fik (got), gik (went)
- Question formation: Spiser du? (inversion — verb first)
- Negation: ikke (not), aldrig (never), ingen (none)

### A2 Vocabulary expansion — everyday life:

**Transport:** bus, tog (train), cykel (bicycle), bil (car), fly (plane)
**Shopping:** butik (shop), pris (price), billig (cheap), dyr (expensive), købe (to buy)
**Time:** i dag (today), i morgen (tomorrow), i går (yesterday), nu (now), snart (soon)
**Weather:** sol (sun), regn (rain), sne (snow), vind (wind), koldt (cold), varmt (warm)
**Home:** køkken (kitchen), stue (living room), soveværelse (bedroom), badeværelse (bathroom)

## B1 — Advanced connections and nuances

### Idiomatic expressions (with English equivalents):
- "Det er lige meget" = "It doesn't matter" (lit. it is equal amount)
- "Hvad så?" = "So what?" / "What's up?"
- "Gå op i røg" = "Go up in smoke" (same idiom!)
- "Slå to fluer med ét smæk" = "Kill two birds with one stone" (lit. kill two flies with one slap)
- "Tage det roligt" = "Take it easy"
- "Det er ikke min kop te" = "It's not my cup of tea" (identical!)
- "Koste en arm og et ben" = "Cost an arm and a leg" (identical structure!)
- "Gøre en bjørnetjeneste" = "Do someone a disservice" (lit. do a bear service)
- "Komme til sagen" = "Get to the point"
- "Slå koldt vand i blodet" = "Cool down / calm down" (lit. pour cold water in the blood)

### B1 Grammar nuances:
- **Subjunctive mood:** "hvis jeg var rig..." (if I were rich) — same as English "were"
- **Passive voice:** Bogen læses (The book is read) vs. Bogen bliver læst (active passive)
- **Modal verbs in past:** kunne (could), måtte (had to), ville (wanted to), skulle (was supposed to)
- **Relative clauses:** "Manden, som/der bor her..." (the man who lives here)
- **Subordinate clause word order:** "Jeg ved, at han ikke kommer" (ikke before verb in sub-clause)

### B1 Register — formal vs. informal:
- De (formal you) vs. du (informal) — De is rare but exists in writing
- "Hvad hedder du?" (informal) vs. "Hvad er Deres navn?" (very formal)
- Written Danish is more conservative than spoken Danish

### False friends — warn the student immediately:

| fart | speed | fart |

| bad | bath | bad (negative) |
| mand | man / husband | man |
| kind | cheek | kind (nice) |

### B1 Common collocations:
- tage en beslutning (make a decision — not "lave")
- gøre en forskel (make a difference)
- have ret (be right — lit. have right)
- være glad for (be happy about)
- synes om (like / be of the opinion)
- komme af med (get rid of)
- holde op med (stop doing something)

### Pronunciation guide for English speakers:
- **Soft d (blødt d):** Between vowels, the Danish d sounds like the English "th" in "the". Example: "mad" (food) is pronounced "math" roughly. Drill: rød, mad, glad, side, bade.
- **Stød (glottal stop):** A creaky break in the voice. "Hund" with stød vs without changes meaning. No English equivalent — closest is the Cockney "bottle" (bo'le).
- **Reduced vowels:** Danish swallows many syllables. "Selvfølgelig" sounds like "sel-fol-li". Train the student to listen rather than read.
- **å sound:** Like the "aw" in "awe". Practice: å, år, gå, stå, kål.
- **ø sound:** Like the "u" in "burn" with rounded lips. Practice: grøn, øl, søster, størrelse.
- **æ sound:** Like the "a" in "bad" but longer. Practice: læse, æble, hæfte.
